## Direct Answer

If you have reliable grid electricity and your state offers net metering, an **on-grid solar system** is the most economical choice. It sends excess power to the grid and reduces your electricity bill through credits. If you live in a remote area without grid access or face 8+ hours of daily power cuts, an **off-grid system** with batteries provides complete independence. If you experience frequent outages but have grid access, a **hybrid solar system** offers the best balance — it uses grid power when available, stores excess in batteries, and automatically switches to battery backup during outages. For most urban Indian homes with 2-4 hour daily cuts, a hybrid system is the practical choice despite higher upfront cost.

## How Each System Works

### On-Grid Solar System

An on-grid system, also called grid-tied or grid-connected, is directly linked to the utility electricity grid. It consists of solar panels, a grid-tie inverter, and a net meter. Panels generate DC electricity during daylight, the inverter converts it to AC at 230V and 50 Hz synchronized with the grid, and the net meter tracks both import and export.

The defining characteristic: **it cannot operate during a grid outage**. When the grid fails, the inverter shuts down within 2 seconds via anti-islanding protection (per IEC 62116) to prevent backfeeding electricity into dead lines and endangering utility workers. Even on a sunny afternoon, an on-grid system produces zero power during a blackout.

During a typical day in Delhi or Mumbai, panels start generating around 6:30 AM, peak between 12:00 PM and 2:00 PM, and taper off by 6:00 PM. If your home consumes 2 kW and panels produce 4 kW, the excess 2 kW flows to the grid through the net meter. At night, you draw power from the grid. At billing cycle end, you pay only for net consumption (import minus export).

**Best for**: Urban and semi-urban homes with stable grid supply and available net metering. Lowest upfront cost, fastest payback (3-5 years), minimal maintenance.

**Limitations**: No power during outages. Dependent on grid quality — severe voltage fluctuations (common in rural UP and Bihar, where we measure 160V-280V instead of standard 230V) can cause frequent inverter disconnections, stopping generation even in full sunlight. Net metering policies vary by state and change frequently, creating uncertainty in long-term savings.

### Off-Grid Solar System

An off-grid system operates completely independently from the utility grid. It is a standalone power generation and storage system designed to meet 100% of energy needs without any grid connection. Essential for remote villages, agricultural pump sets, telecom towers, and locations where grid extension is economically unviable.

Components include solar panels sized for daily consumption plus battery charging, an MPPT charge controller (preferred in India for higher efficiency under varying irradiance), a battery bank typically sized for 1-3 days of autonomy, and a pure sine wave off-grid inverter for sensitive electronics. A backup diesel generator is often included for extended cloudy periods.

During the day, panels charge batteries while running loads. At night, the inverter draws from batteries. During monsoon when generation drops 30-40%, the battery bank must bridge the gap. If state of charge drops critically, a diesel generator automatically starts.

**Best for**: Remote locations without grid access or where grid reliability is extremely poor. Complete energy independence, no electricity bills, no net metering required.

**Limitations**: Highest upfront cost (2-3x on-grid equivalent). Batteries constitute 40-60% of system cost and require replacement every 5-15 years depending on chemistry. System must be oversized for consecutive cloudy days (monsoon), increasing panel and battery requirements. Running heavy loads like air conditioners or industrial motors requires very large battery banks, making off-grid impractical for high-consumption applications.

### Hybrid Solar System

A hybrid system combines the best of both worlds: grid connectivity plus battery storage. It connects to the utility grid while incorporating batteries, allowing you to store excess solar energy, sell surplus to the grid, and maintain power during outages.

The **hybrid solar inverter** is the brain. It manages solar panels, battery bank, and grid simultaneously, prioritizing power flow: loads first, then battery charging, then grid export. During outages, it switches to battery power within 20 milliseconds — imperceptible to most appliances. Advanced models optimize for time-of-use tariffs, charging batteries from grid during low-tariff hours and discharging during peak hours.

In a typical urban Indian home in Bengaluru or Chennai with a 5 kW hybrid system: panels start at 7:00 AM, power runs loads and charges batteries. By 10:00 AM, if batteries are full and solar exceeds demand, excess exports to the grid for net metering credits. At 6:00 PM, solar stops and grid takes over. During a sudden 8:00 PM power cut, the inverter instantly switches to battery without interrupting sensitive electronics.

**Best for**: Urban homes with frequent power cuts (2-6 hours daily), commercial buildings with high demand charges, and factories requiring 24/7 operations. Offers backup power, peak shaving, and time-of-use arbitrage.

**Limitations**: Highest system cost (60-80% more than on-grid equivalent). Battery replacement is a major future expense — budget Rs. 1.5-2.5 lakhs for a 5 kW system after 12-15 years. Multiple energy conversions (DC solar to DC battery to AC loads) introduce 8-15% cumulative efficiency losses compared to direct on-grid systems. Complex installation requires technicians skilled in both grid-tie and battery systems.

## Engineering Insights: What We See in the Field

### Voltage Fluctuations

India's distribution grid is notorious for voltage instability. In rural Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, we routinely measure 160V-280V instead of the standard 230V. Grid-tie inverters have voltage windows (typically 180V-270V) outside which they disconnect for safety. During low-voltage periods, on-grid systems stop generating entirely — a frustrating experience for owners who see full sunlight but zero output.

Our solution: For severe voltage issues, we recommend hybrid systems with wide-voltage inverters or voltage stabilizers specifically for the inverter. In extreme cases, we design off-grid systems with grid input as a backup charger rather than primary source.

### High Temperatures

Solar panel efficiency decreases as temperature rises — typically 0.4-0.5% per degree Celsius above 25 degrees C. In Rajasthan and Gujarat where ambient temperatures reach 48-50 degrees C, panel surface temperatures can exceed 75 degrees C. A 550W panel rated at standard test conditions may produce only 460-480W in peak summer.

We specify panels with low temperature coefficients (-0.35% per degree C or better). Proper mounting with 15-20 cm clearance from roof surface allows airflow cooling. Light-colored roofs or reflective coatings reduce ambient temperature. Inverter placement in shaded, ventilated areas prevents thermal derating.

### Dust and Soiling

Northern India, particularly Delhi NCR and the Indo-Gangetic plain, experiences severe dust accumulation. During summer months, we observe 15-25% output reduction within two weeks of cleaning. After dust storms, panels can lose 40-50% output within days.

Manual cleaning with soft water and squeegees every 7-10 days is essential during dry seasons. Automated cleaning systems are viable above 100 kW. Tilt angle matters — panels at 25-30 degrees shed dust better than flat installations. Anti-dust coatings are emerging but not yet proven for long-term Indian conditions.

### Humidity and Monsoon

Coastal regions (Mumbai, Chennai, Kochi) and the Northeast face high humidity and heavy rainfall. We have observed corrosion of mounting structures within 3-4 years when standard galvanized steel is used instead of hot-dip galvanized (HDG) or aluminum.

Water ingress into junction boxes is a recurring issue. Humidity above 85% can cause condensation inside inverters if ventilation is inadequate. Our standard: IP65-rated junction boxes, double-sealed cable glands, and inverter installation in weatherproof enclosures with active ventilation.

Monsoon generation drops by 30-50%. For off-grid systems, battery autonomy must be designed for 2-3 days of cloudy weather. For hybrid systems, battery capacity must account for reduced solar charging during extended monsoon periods.

### Cable Sizing and Earthing

Undersized cables are the most common installation mistake we encounter. Voltage drop in DC cables should not exceed 1-2%. For a 5 kW system with panels at 15 meters distance, 4 sq.mm cables are insufficient — we specify 6-10 sq.mm depending on distance and current. We use only copper conductors, not aluminum.

Proper earthing is non-negotiable. Indian soil conditions vary dramatically — rocky Deccan plateau has high resistivity, while alluvial plains have low resistivity. We measure earth resistance at every installation and achieve less than 5 ohms using chemical earthing electrodes or multiple earth pits. Lightning protection is essential: India has one of the highest lightning strike densities globally. We install Class B+C lightning arrestors on every installation with surge protection devices at both DC and AC sides.

### Installation Mistakes We Frequently Correct

**Shading**: Even partial shading from a water pipe or neighboring building can reduce string output by 20-30%. We use shade analysis tools before finalizing panel layout.

**Inverter Oversizing**: Some installers pair 10 kW inverters with 5 kW panels to "allow for future expansion." This reduces inverter efficiency at low loads and wastes capital. We size inverter capacity at 100-110% of panel capacity.

**Battery Undersizing**: In hybrid systems, batteries sized only for 4-hour backup cannot absorb peak solar generation. This forces export or curtailment, wasting capacity. We size batteries to absorb at least 50% of daily solar generation.

**Poor Roof Assessment**: Many installers fail to assess structural load capacity. Wet concrete roofs with 20-year-old waterproofing may not support 20-25 kg per sq.m additional load. We conduct structural assessments and recommend reinforcement where needed.

### Real Maintenance Observations

After monitoring hundreds of installations across India, we observe consistent patterns:

- Systems with quarterly professional maintenance perform 12-18% better than those with only annual checks
- Inverters installed in direct sunlight fail 3x faster than those in shaded locations
- Lead-acid batteries in unventilated enclosures lose 40% capacity within 2 years
- Systems with proper monitoring identify issues 2-3 months earlier than unmonitored systems
- Cable joints and MC4 connectors are the most common failure points after 5+ years

## Common Myths

### Myth 1: Solar Panels Work Better in Hot Weather

**Reality**: Solar panels convert light, not heat. Higher temperatures reduce efficiency. A panel at 50 degrees C produces 8-12% less electricity than the same panel at 25 degrees C. What matters is solar irradiance (light intensity), not ambient temperature. Cool, sunny days produce more electricity than hot, sunny days.

### Myth 2: On-Grid Systems Provide Backup Power During Outages

**Reality**: Standard on-grid systems shut down during grid outages as a safety requirement. Anti-islanding protection prevents electricity from flowing back into the grid and endangering utility workers. If you need backup power, you need a hybrid system with batteries or a separate UPS.

### Myth 3: Solar Systems Do Not Work During Monsoon or Cloudy Days

**Reality**: Solar panels generate electricity even under diffuse light. During overcast conditions, output drops to 10-30% of rated capacity — but they still produce power. Modern panels with high low-light performance maintain 15-20% output even in heavy cloud cover. The annual impact of monsoon is factored into system design.

### Myth 4: Off-Grid Systems Are Cheaper Than Paying Electricity Bills

**Reality**: Off-grid systems have the highest upfront cost and ongoing battery replacement expenses. For locations with grid access, payback is typically 6-10 years compared to 3-5 years for on-grid. Off-grid is economically justified only when grid extension costs exceed solar plus battery costs, or when grid reliability is extremely poor.

### Myth 5: Any Solar Installer Can Install Any Type of System

**Reality**: Off-grid and hybrid systems require specialized knowledge of battery chemistry, charge controllers, inverter programming, and safety protocols. We have encountered numerous installations where general electrical contractors installed hybrid systems incorrectly — wrong charging voltages, improper ventilation, inadequate cable sizing, and missing safety disconnects. Always verify installer experience with your specific system type.

### Myth 6: Adding Batteries to an Existing On-Grid System is Simple

**Reality**: Retrofitting batteries is often complex and sometimes impossible. Standard grid-tie inverters cannot charge batteries or operate off-grid. You need either a hybrid inverter replacement or an AC-coupled battery system with a separate battery inverter — both requiring significant rewiring and cost. If you anticipate needing batteries within 3-5 years, install a hybrid inverter from the start.

## Common Buying Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Choosing System Type Based on Budget Alone

The cheapest option (on-grid) may leave you without power during outages. The most expensive (off-grid) may be unnecessary if you have reliable grid access. Evaluate actual needs: grid reliability, outage frequency, critical loads, and long-term plans. A hybrid system costs more upfront but may provide better value if you experience frequent cuts or plan to add electric vehicles.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Local Grid Conditions

A homeowner in Gurgaon with stable grid and net metering should choose differently from a factory owner in rural Maharashtra with 6-hour daily power cuts. Visit your local electricity office, understand net metering availability, and assess actual grid reliability before deciding.

### Mistake 3: Undersizing the System

Many buyers install systems meeting only 50-60% of consumption to save money. This leads to disappointment when bills remain high. Size for 80-100% of daytime consumption for on-grid, or 100% of total daily consumption for off-grid. Use your last 12 months of electricity bills for accurate sizing.

### Mistake 4: Overlooking Battery Quality

In hybrid and off-grid systems, battery quality determines success. Cheap lead-acid batteries may cost half as much as LiFePO4 but require replacement twice as often, have lower depth of discharge, and perform poorly in Indian heat. Over 10 years, cheap batteries often cost more than quality batteries. Read our [LiFePO4 vs Lead Acid Battery Guide](/blog/lifepo4-vs-lead-acid-batteries) for detailed comparison.

### Mistake 5: Not Planning for Future Expansion

Your energy needs will grow. You may add air conditioners, electric vehicles, or expand your factory. Install conduit pipes and leave space for additional panels. Choose an inverter with 20-30% capacity headroom. If installing a hybrid system, size the battery bank to allow future parallel expansion.

### Mistake 6: Choosing Installer Based on Lowest Price

Solar installation requires electrical expertise, structural knowledge, and safety compliance. The lowest bidder often cuts corners on cable quality, earthing, mounting structure gauge, or inverter placement. Verify installer certifications, past installations, and warranty terms. A poorly installed system may cost more to fix than a properly installed one.

### Mistake 7: Expecting Immediate Bill Reduction

Net metering credits may take 1-3 billing cycles to reflect. System performance improves after initial "burn-in" period. Dust accumulation in first weeks may mask actual generation. Set realistic expectations and monitor for 3-6 months before judging performance.

## How to Choose the Right System

So which system is right for your situation? Follow this framework:

### Step 1: Assess Grid Reliability

Track your power outages for one month:
- **0-2 hours daily cuts**: On-grid system is sufficient
- **2-6 hours daily cuts**: Hybrid system recommended
- **6+ hours daily cuts or no grid**: Off-grid system required

### Step 2: Check Net Metering Availability

Contact your DISCOM or visit their website. Verify:
- Is net metering available for your consumer category?
- What is the maximum capacity allowed?
- What is the feed-in tariff or credit mechanism?
- Are there application fees or security deposits?

States with favorable net metering (as of 2026): Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Rajasthan. States with restrictions: Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, West Bengal (check current status as policies change frequently).

### Step 3: Calculate Energy Needs

Review your last 12 months of electricity bills. Calculate average monthly consumption, peak summer consumption, daytime vs nighttime split, and critical loads that must run during outages. For rough sizing: 1 kW solar generates 4-5 kWh per day in India. A 5 kW system generates 20-25 kWh daily, sufficient for most 3-4 BHK homes with moderate AC usage.

### Step 4: Evaluate Roof or Land

1 kW requires approximately 8-10 sq.m of shadow-free area. South-facing is ideal; east-west requires 10-15% more panels. 20-30 degrees tilt is optimal for most Indian latitudes. Ensure no shading between 9 AM and 4 PM. Verify roof can support 15-20 kg per sq.m additional load — older buildings (20+ years) may need reinforcement.

### Step 5: Determine Budget and Financing

- **On-grid**: Rs. 60,000-80,000 per kW (including installation)
- **Hybrid**: Rs. 100,000-140,000 per kW (including LiFePO4 batteries)
- **Off-grid**: Rs. 120,000-180,000 per kW (including batteries and generator)

Factor in PM Surya Ghar subsidy (up to Rs. 78,000 for residential systems up to 3 kW), state-specific subsidies, bank loans at 8-10% interest, and 40% accelerated depreciation for commercial installations.

### Step 6: Consider Future Needs

Planning to buy an electric vehicle? Add 10-15 kWh daily consumption per vehicle. Expanding home or factory? Size conduit and inverter for 30% expansion. Adding battery storage later? Install hybrid inverter from the start. Moving to a new location? On-grid systems add property value; off-grid systems do not transfer easily.

### Step 7: Choose the Right Installer

Verify MNRE empanelment or state nodal agency registration, minimum 50 successful installations of your system type, manufacturer certifications, structural engineer assessment for roof-mounted systems, warranty terms, after-sales service commitment, and remote monitoring capability.

## Installation Tips

### Pre-Installation Checklist

1. **Structural Assessment**: Engage a structural engineer to certify roof load capacity. For buildings older than 20 years, inspect concrete and waterproofing condition.
2. **Electrical Assessment**: Verify existing electrical panel capacity, earthing condition, and cable routing paths. Upgrade panel if necessary.
3. **Shade Analysis**: Use Solar Pathfinder or drone-based analysis to identify shading patterns throughout the year. Account for future tree growth.
4. **Permits and Approvals**: Obtain net metering approval from DISCOM before installation. Some states require structural approval from municipal authorities.
5. **Material Inspection**: Verify panel wattage, inverter specifications, cable gauge, and mounting structure material match your purchase order. Check manufacturing dates — panels older than 2 years from inventory may have degraded.

### Best Practices

**Mounting Structure**: Use hot-dip galvanized (HDG) steel or aluminum with minimum 80-micron zinc coating. Maintain 15-20 cm clearance between panel back and roof for ventilation. Ensure proper waterproofing at all roof penetrations. Design for wind speeds appropriate to your region (150 km/h coastal, 130 km/h inland).

**Panel Installation**: Install in portrait orientation for better self-cleaning. Use MC4 connectors with proper crimping tools. Leave 5-10 mm gap between panels for thermal expansion. Ground all panel frames and mounting structures.

**Inverter Placement**: Install in shaded, ventilated locations — never in direct sunlight or enclosed spaces without ventilation. Maintain 30 cm clearance on all sides. For outdoor installation, use IP65-rated inverters with sun shields.

**Battery Installation**: LiFePO4 batteries go in ventilated indoor locations at 15-35 degrees C, away from direct sunlight. Lead-acid batteries require well-ventilated enclosures (hydrogen gas release) — never in living spaces. Maintain 10-15 cm spacing between batteries. Install BMS for lithium batteries to prevent overcharge, over-discharge, and thermal runaway.

**Cable Routing**: Use UV-resistant, double-insulated DC cables for outdoor runs. Run DC and AC cables in separate conduits. Label all cables at both ends. Use cable ties every 50 cm. Avoid sharp bends (minimum bend radius 8x cable diameter).

**Earthing and Lightning Protection**: Install separate earth pits for DC, AC, and lightning protection. Bond all earth pits. Use copper-clad steel or pure copper electrodes. Maintain earth resistance below 5 ohms. Install Class B+C lightning arrestor at building entry point. Add Type 2 SPD at inverter DC input and AC output. Test earth resistance before and after monsoon season.

### Post-Installation Checks

1. **Insulation Resistance Test**: Verify greater than 1 M-ohm between DC conductors and ground.
2. **Open Circuit Voltage Test**: Verify panel string voltage matches design calculations.
3. **Short Circuit Current Test**: Verify current matches panel specifications under test conditions.
4. **Inverter Commissioning**: Program correct grid parameters, battery charging voltages, and export limits.
5. **Net Meter Installation**: Coordinate with DISCOM for net meter installation and commissioning.
6. **Monitoring Setup**: Configure remote monitoring portal, set alert thresholds, and verify data transmission.
7. **User Training**: Demonstrate system operation, monitoring app usage, emergency shutdown procedures, and basic troubleshooting.

## Cost Factors

### System Type Impact

**On-Grid**: Driven by panel quality, inverter capacity, and installation complexity. No battery cost means lower upfront investment but no backup capability.

**Off-Grid**: Battery cost dominates — 40-60% of total system cost. Oversizing for 2-3 days autonomy increases panel and battery requirements. Generator adds Rs. 30,000-80,000.

**Hybrid**: Combines both approaches. Hybrid inverters cost 30-50% more than standard grid-tie. Battery cost adds significantly. Time-of-use optimization and peak shaving improve economics in commercial applications.

### Component Quality

**Panels**: Tier 1 (Jinko, LONGi, Trina Solar, Waaree) cost 10-15% more than Tier 2/3 but offer better efficiency, lower degradation, and stronger warranties. For a 5 kW system, the difference is Rs. 15,000-25,000 — justified by 25-year performance.

**Inverters**: String inverters (Rs. 3-5 per watt) vs microinverters (Rs. 6-8 per watt). Hybrid inverters (Rs. 8-12 per watt) are most expensive. European brands (SMA, Fronius) cost 20-30% more but offer better service networks.

**Batteries**: LiFePO4 costs Rs. 25,000-35,000 per kWh. Lead-acid costs Rs. 12,000-18,000 per kWh but requires 2x capacity for equivalent usable energy (50% depth of discharge vs 80-90% for LiFePO4). Over 10 years, LiFePO4 is more economical. Read our [Home Solar Battery Savings Guide](/blog/home-solar-battery-savings) for detailed analysis.

**Mounting Structures**: Hot-dip galvanized steel costs 30-40% more than standard galvanized but lasts 20+ years vs 8-10 years. Aluminum costs 50% more but is corrosion-proof and lightweight.

### Installation Complexity

- **Roof Type**: RCC flat roofs require tilted structures (Rs. 8-12 per watt). Tin sheds use clamp-based mounting (Rs. 5-8 per watt). Tile roofs need specialized hooks (Rs. 10-15 per watt).
- **Height**: Multi-story installations above 3rd floor cost 10-20% more due to additional labor and safety equipment.
- **Cable Runs**: Long distances between panels and inverter increase cable cost and voltage drop. Underground routing adds excavation costs.
- **Electrical Upgrades**: If your existing panel cannot accommodate solar integration, upgrading adds Rs. 15,000-40,000.
- **Battery Room**: For off-grid/hybrid systems, preparing a ventilated, temperature-controlled battery room may require construction work.

### Location Factors

- **Labor**: Metro cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru) have higher labor rates than tier-2/3 cities. Remote rural locations may have higher transport costs.
- **Permits**: Some states charge net metering application fees, inspection fees, or security deposits (Rs. 0 to Rs. 50,000).
- **Weather**: Coastal areas need higher-grade corrosion protection. High-wind regions need stronger mounting. Desert areas need more frequent cleaning infrastructure.

### Scale Economics

- **Residential (3-10 kW)**: Rs. 60,000-90,000 per kW. Higher per-watt cost due to fixed overheads.
- **Commercial (50-500 kW)**: Rs. 45,000-65,000 per kW. Economies of scale reduce costs.
- **Industrial (1 MW+)**: Rs. 35,000-50,000 per kW. Bulk procurement and standardized installation reduce costs significantly.

### Hidden Costs to Budget For

- Net Metering Application: Rs. 0-50,000 depending on state
- Electrical Panel Upgrade: Rs. 15,000-40,000 if needed
- Roof Waterproofing Renewal: Rs. 20,000-60,000 if aged
- Tree Trimming: Rs. 5,000-20,000 if shading removal needed

## Government Schemes and Regulations

### PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana

Launched in 2024, the flagship residential solar subsidy scheme:
- **Subsidy Amount**: Rs. 30,000 per kW for first 3 kW; Rs. 18,000 per kW for additional capacity up to 10 kW. Maximum Rs. 78,000 for 3 kW systems.
- **Eligibility**: Residential consumers with valid electricity connections. Group housing societies can get up to Rs. 9,000 per kW for common facilities up to 500 kW.
- **Application**: Through national portal (pmsuryaghar.gov.in). DISCOM verifies, installs net meter, and credits subsidy after commissioning.
- **Mandatory**: Must use MNRE-approved components, install net metering, and use empaneled vendors.
- **Timeline**: Applications processed within 30 days. Installation completed within 90 days of approval.

**Important**: Subsidy rates and eligibility change periodically. Always verify current rates on the official portal before budgeting.

### MNRE Guidelines

- **Quality Standards**: MNRE maintains an Approved List of Models and Manufacturers (ALMM). Only ALMM-approved components are eligible for subsidies.
- **Net Metering Framework**: MNRE has issued model regulations, but implementation varies by state. Most states allow net metering up to 1 MW.
- **Rooftop Solar Targets**: National target of 40 GW rooftop solar by 2026.
- **Solar Parks**: For large installations, MNRE facilitates solar parks with ready infrastructure and single-window clearance.

### Net Metering Regulations

Determined by State Electricity Regulatory Commissions (SERCs):

**Billing Mechanisms**:
- **Gross Metering**: All solar generation exported at feed-in tariff; all consumption billed at retail rate. Less favorable.
- **Net Metering**: Export and import netted at retail rate. Most favorable.
- **Net Billing**: Export compensated at lower feed-in tariff (not retail rate). Import billed at retail rate.

**Capacity Limits**: Most states allow up to sanctioned load or 1 MW, whichever is lower. Some have lower caps (e.g., 50% of sanctioned load).

**Banking Period**: Annual banking is most common; some states have monthly or quarterly settlement.

**Important**: Regulations change frequently. Some states have temporarily suspended net metering or reduced feed-in tariffs. Always verify current regulations with your DISCOM before installation.

### Regulatory Compliance

- **CEA Technical Standards**: Grid-connected systems must comply with Central Electricity Authority standards.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### 1. What is the difference between on-grid, off-grid, and hybrid solar systems?

On-grid connects to the utility grid and exports excess power but shuts down during outages. Off-grid operates independently with batteries and no grid connection. Hybrid connects to the grid while storing energy in batteries for backup during outages. The choice depends on grid availability, reliability, and backup needs.

### 2. Which solar system is best for Indian homes?

For most urban homes with reliable grid and net metering, on-grid is most economical. For homes with frequent power cuts (2-6 hours daily), hybrid provides the best balance. For rural homes without grid access, off-grid is the only option.

### 3. How does net metering work in India?

A bidirectional meter tracks electricity imported from and exported to the grid. At billing cycle end, you pay only for net consumption (import minus export). Credits carry forward based on your state's banking period. Policies vary — some states offer 1:1 credit, others offer lower feed-in tariffs. Apply through your DISCOM.

### 4. Do I need batteries for my solar system?

Batteries are mandatory only for off-grid systems. On-grid systems do not need batteries. Hybrid systems include batteries for backup during outages. If your area has stable grid supply, an on-grid system without batteries is the most cost-effective choice.

### 5. What happens during a power outage with an on-grid system?

The system shuts down completely. Grid-tie inverters have anti-islanding protection that disconnects during grid failures to prevent backfeeding electricity into dead lines, which could endanger utility workers. Even in full sunlight, you will have no solar power during an outage with a standard on-grid system.

### 6. How much does a hybrid solar system cost in India?

A residential hybrid system costs approximately Rs. 100,000-140,000 per kW including LiFePO4 batteries, installation, and commissioning. A typical 5 kW hybrid system costs Rs. 5-7 lakhs. Commercial systems (50 kW+) cost Rs. 70,000-100,000 per kW due to economies of scale.

### 7. Can I run my factory on solar power alone?

Yes, depending on your grid situation. With grid access, a large on-grid or hybrid system can offset most consumption. For 24/7 operations, a hybrid system with substantial battery storage handles outages. For very large factories (1 MW+), on-grid is most economical. Off-grid is generally impractical for large factories due to massive battery requirements.

### 8. What is the lifespan of a solar battery in Indian conditions?

LiFePO4 batteries last 10-15 years with 4,000-6,000 cycles at 80% depth of discharge. Lead-acid lasts 5-7 years with 800-1,200 cycles at 50% depth of discharge. Indian heat accelerates degradation — batteries in unventilated enclosures may lose 30-40% of expected lifespan. Proper installation in cool, ventilated spaces is critical. Read our [LiFePO4 vs Lead Acid Battery Guide](/blog/lifepo4-vs-lead-acid-batteries) for detailed comparison.

### 9. Is off-grid solar viable for urban apartments?

Generally no. Apartments face limited roof access (shared among residents), space constraints for batteries, and usually stable grid supply. Better suited for on-grid systems if individual metering is possible, or community solar where the housing society installs a shared system.

### 10. How does monsoon affect solar system performance?

Solar generation drops 30-50% during monsoon due to cloud cover. However, panels still generate 10-30% of rated capacity even under heavy clouds. For on-grid systems, the grid compensates naturally. For off-grid systems, battery autonomy must be designed for 2-3 days of low generation. For hybrid systems, batteries bridge the gap.

### 11. What size solar system do I need for a 3BHK house?

A typical 3BHK consumes 15-25 kWh per day. A 5 kW system generates 20-25 kWh daily, sufficient for most 3BHK homes with moderate usage (2 ACs, refrigerator, washing machine, lights, fans). Heavy usage (3+ ACs, water heater, EV) requires 7-10 kW. Use your last 12 months of electricity bills for accurate sizing.

### 12. Can I add batteries to my existing on-grid system later?

Yes, but it is complex and expensive. Standard grid-tie inverters cannot charge batteries or operate off-grid. You need either a hybrid inverter replacement or an AC-coupled battery system with a separate battery inverter — both requiring significant rewiring and cost. If you anticipate needing batteries within 3-5 years, install a hybrid inverter from the start.

### 13. What is a hybrid solar inverter and how does it work?

It is an intelligent device managing multiple power sources simultaneously: solar panels, battery bank, and utility grid. It converts DC solar power to AC for your loads, charges batteries from solar or grid, and synchronizes with the grid for net metering. During outages, it instantly switches to battery power (within 20 ms). Advanced models optimize energy flow based on time-of-use tariffs and solar generation forecasts. Read our [Hybrid Solar Inverter Guide](/blog/hybrid-solar-inverter-guide) for detailed explanation.

### 14. Are solar systems worth it in areas with frequent power cuts?

Yes, but choose the right type. On-grid systems are frustrating because they shut down during outages. Hybrid systems are ideal — they provide grid savings when power is available and battery backup during cuts. The additional battery cost is justified by reliability and convenience.

### 15. What government subsidies are available for solar in India?

The primary scheme is PM Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana offering up to Rs. 78,000 for residential systems up to 3 kW. Additional Rs. 18,000 per kW for 3-10 kW systems. Group housing societies can get up to Rs. 9,000 per kW for common facilities. Commercial installations can claim 40% accelerated depreciation. Check the official PM Surya Ghar portal and your state nodal agency for current rates.

## Key Takeaways

- **On-grid systems** are the most economical choice for urban and semi-urban areas with reliable grid supply and available net metering. They offer the fastest payback period (3-5 years) but provide no power during outages.

- **Off-grid systems** provide complete energy independence and are essential for remote locations without grid access. They require significant battery investment and have the highest upfront cost, but eliminate electricity bills entirely.

- **Hybrid systems** offer the best balance for Indian conditions where grid reliability is moderate and power cuts are common. They combine grid savings with battery backup, making them the most versatile option for urban homes and commercial buildings.

- **Indian conditions** — high temperatures, dust, monsoon, humidity, and voltage fluctuations — significantly impact system design, component selection, and maintenance requirements. Systems designed for temperate climates will underperform in India.

- **Battery chemistry choice** between LiFePO4 and lead-acid dramatically affects long-term economics. LiFePO4 batteries cost more upfront but last 2-3x longer and perform better in Indian heat, making them more economical over the system lifetime.

- **Proper installation** is critical: correct cable sizing, robust earthing, lightning protection, adequate ventilation, and professional commissioning determine whether a system performs for 25 years or fails within 5 years.

- **Government schemes** like PM Surya Ghar can reduce residential system costs by 40-60%. Always verify current subsidy rates and net metering policies before installation, as regulations change frequently.

- **System sizing** must account for actual consumption patterns, seasonal variations, and future expansion needs. Undersized systems disappoint; oversized systems waste capital. Use 12 months of electricity data for accurate sizing.

- **Maintenance is not optional**: Quarterly professional checks and monthly panel cleaning are essential. Systems with regular maintenance outperform neglected systems by 12-18% annually.

- **Installer selection matters**: Verify MNRE empanelment, past installations, manufacturer certifications, and warranty terms. The lowest bid often costs more in the long run.
